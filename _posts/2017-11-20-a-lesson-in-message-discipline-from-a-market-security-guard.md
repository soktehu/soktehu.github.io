“As security guards, we’re concerned with three things: safety, order, and comfort,” said the head security guard of Palmerah Market between puffs of his cigarette. “I’m sure you can elaborate from there.”

He sat in a black swivel armchair at the front of the security post, which was tucked away in the back corner of the market, underneath a flight of stairs. The only light in the room came from the wisps of sunlight streaming through the tinted windows, the glare of the TV in the opposite corner, and the embers of his lit cigarette.

He wore a stylish blue T-shirt, slim square glasses, and an air of supreme confidence. He had been working there for 14 years, he said. And in those 14 years, nothing bad had ever happened on his watch. Ever.

Except maybe the occasional pickpocket, he conceded. “But there are pickpockets everywhere, right?” he said. “Even in the malls.”

What did the security guards do when they caught a pickpocket? “Oh, we process them according to the law,” he answered, without hesitation. “No hanky panky.”

What were the hours like? “Twelve hours, just like the Ministry of Manpower allows,” he said, smirking. “Everything is by-the-book here.”

He declined to give his name, citing prior bad experiences with journalists. It was the only time he let himself get worked up.

“One time, some reporter wrote about how messy it is out there,” he grumbled, gesturing to the sidewalk with his cigarette. Fruit sellers from the market often spill onto the street in the mornings, causing traffic. “Some of the guys got into trouble, even though that’s not our responsibility.”

But nothing bad had ever happened, right? So how could he get into trouble? “These days, people can get into trouble for anything,” he replied easily. “Look at those guys making Setya Novanto memes.”

He pivoted then to a discussion about the merits and failings of journalism, paying particular attention to the latter.

“Reporters will write anything they want,” he said. He mimed someone typing away on a keyboard. “They’ll write ‘so-and-so said this and that’ and then it’s in the paper and I’ll be in trouble with my boss.”

He was quick to add, though, that he had great relationships with the many journalists that work in the area — many print and online news publications are headquartered in Palmerah.

“Lots of those Kompas guys know me,” he said, motioning towards the direction of the Kompas office. “I’ve forgotten their names, but they’ve all come to me and asked me about things.”

“You know Pak Ojong?” he asked, referring to Kompas co-founder Peter Kanisius Ojong. “I knew him. I used to intern there a long time ago.” He didn’t continue that career path, he said, because he still wanted to do “other things.”

He name-dropped a few other Indonesian journalistic notables before allowing himself to be guided back to the topic of being a security guard.

What was the daily grind like? “Like I said, safety, order, and comfort,” he reiterated. “That’s what we do.”

But what exactly was it that he did when he came into work every day? “What do you do when you go to work?” he countered. “It’s all the same.”

Did he assign the other guards with different tasks? “You see, we have SOPs [Standard Operating Procedures] here,” he explained. “So everyone knows what to do. I don’t have to tell them.”

So what were the SOPs? “What’s the SOP for being a journalist?” he said reflexively, before going back to his favorite phrase. “Safety, order, and comfort. That’s really all there is. You can expand from there, right?” He smiled benevolently.

He took another drag from the cigarette and “That’s enough? You have what you need?” he asked, looking ready to offer more safety, order, and comfort if necessary. He leaned back into his chair.

“Come back if you ever need anything else,” he said, still smiling. “Take care.”
