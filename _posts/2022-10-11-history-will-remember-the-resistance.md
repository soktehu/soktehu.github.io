The contradiction between predestination and free will is a philosophical concept that has always fascinated me. Billions of people believe that, whether through the laws of God or physics, the future is already written. And yet, they simultaneously believe — or at least act as if they do — that their choices matter and can have an impact on the world.

There’s one line in The Lord of the Rings that has stuck with me because of how neatly it illustrates this contradiction. As she greets the Fellowship, Galadriel, speaking about her husband Celeborn, says,

> “… together through ages of the world we have fought the long defeat.”

That line struck 12-year-old me as deeply profound. Galadriel, older than the sun and the moon, believed that her side, in the end, are fated to lose. And yet, this did not seem to deter her from continuing the fight anyway.

When I first met Maria Catarina Sumarsih at a Kamisan protest commemorating the death of labor activist Marsinah, I was struck by the same feeling. There was barely any hope of any past human rights violations being resolved under the current administration, she said, whether it be the death of Marsinah or that of her own son, Wawan. And yet, there she stood, and there she would stand again the next Thursday, and the one after that, and the one after that.

In my darkest moods, this persistence in the face of such studied indifference — heroic as it is — feels like an exercise in futility. What’s the point? Why fight, if nothing changes? If the end is already written, a foregone conclusion?

Intellectually, I know that change mostly happens incrementally, that the time horizon for success is measured in decades. But there’s a difference between knowing something and believing it in your bones, which I’m not sure if I do.

And yet — every once in a while, I hear a song, or watch a speech, or read an article, or attend an event and I feel reassured that whatever else, one thing is true: History will remember the Resistance.

Even if victory takes 100 years — or as in Galadriel’s case, more than 6,000 — the things that we do today will be a testament for posterity. Yes, such and such legislation may pass, despite the thousands of words we write against it; yes, the state may continue to kill with impunity, no matter how loudly we scream for justice.

But in the final reckoning, we can point to every rushed press release, every tiny protest, every line of poetry, every position paper, every Thursday we stood outside under black umbrellas. We can say that we did our part, as Martin Luther King Jr. said, to bend the long arc of the moral universe toward justice.
