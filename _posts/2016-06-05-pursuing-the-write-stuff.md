***

Wind gusts of up to 40 miles per hour, sidewalks buried in over a foot of snow, closed roads, suspended bus lines, dire warnings from government officials. Such a scenario would stop most people from venturing outside on the weekend of January 22nd, 2016, but not a self-described fountain pen obsessive.

Anni Lei, a 25-year-old graduate student at the University of Pennsylvania, trudged through what meteorologists called a “potentially historic blizzard” to get to the 2016 Philadelphia Pen Show.

“I’m a crazy fan,” said Lei. “I never doubted or worried” about the storm.

It was Lei’s first pen show. A native of China, she came to Philadelphia the previous summer to study landscape architecture at Penn’s School of Design. She had used fountain pens as a child, but only got into the hobby a year ago, through her use of elegant and pricey leather daily planners. A friend had told her, “’if you’re using nice planners, you should get a fountain pen.’” Now she has more than 35.

Within two hours of her snowy arrival, Lei had spent more than $5,000 buying six pens.

The storm was still at full blast when Lei and her friend and classmate, Echo Zhao, arrived at the Westin Philadelphia Saturday morning. Bundled in frumpy grey parkas and matching heavy snow boots, Lei and Zhao gave the ballroom one preliminary go-round, as pen show veterans recommend.

Their first significant stop was the Franklin-Christoph encampment, showcasing special editions of pens in different acrylics and offering 100 bottles of limited edition Benjamin Franklin Green ink. Franklin-Christoph, a North Carolina manufacturer and retailer, was one of the show’s busiest vendors.

When customers picked a pen for purchase, they weren’t just handed merchandise. Their name was put on a list to receive the full Franklin-Christoph experience. Once their turn came, they would sit down with Jim Rouse, director of operations and resident “nibmeister,” who screwed the nibs of their choice onto their purchases and offered to fill them with Franklin-Christoph ink in colors ranging from black cherry to midnight emerald to that exclusive Ben Franklin green. Buyers could try out the pen on a notepad while Rouse observed how they wrote with it. Nib too scratchy? Flow too dry? Rouse adjusted the pen until they were perfectly happy.

Lei bought two Franklin-Christoph pens, both made of what the company calls “antique glass,” a transparent green-tinged plastic that brings to mind Coke bottles from the 1950s. These pens, normally only available through a waitlist on the company website, were hot sellers, said owner-proprietor Scott Franklin. At around $140 per pen, these were Lei’s most modest purchases of the day.

***

Lewis Edson Waterman, a New York insurance salesman, first patented the capillary fountain pen in 1884. Fountain pens became the main writing instrument for the next seven decades or so, but by the 1960s the cheaper, more convenient ballpoint pen had taken over.

“In every country, manufacturers of fountain pens were forced to close,” wrote Giorgio Dragoni, an Italian lawyer and pen collector, in his 1998 book “Fountain Pens: History and Design.”

The long-standing New York retailer Fountain Pen Hospital, for example, opened on Warren Street in downtown Manhattan in 1946. By the 1970s, says owner Steve Wiederlight, fountain pen sales were so slow that the Hospital branched out into office supplies. But by the 1990s the trend had reversed itself: office supply sales declined while fountain pen sales grew.

Anderson Pens, a Wisconsin retailer that pen collectors Brian and Lisa Anderson opened in 2010, has also felt the industry’s growth. “It’s been on the rise, absolutely. We’ve never had a down year,” said Brian Anderson. “We’ve had probably 50–100 percent growth year-on-year every year since we’ve started.”

The number of fountain pen companies, blogs and websites that have sprung up since the late 1990s shows that Anderson isn’t unusual. Franklin-Christoph opened its doors in 2001. One of the country’s most popular online pen retailers, The Goulet Pen Company, started in 2009.

Internationally, the Taiwanese manufacturer TWSBI has been an instant success since the launch of its flagship model, the TWSBI 540, in 2009. Reddit, one of the top 10 most visited websites in the US, started a [sub-forum](https://www.reddit.com/r/fountainpens) dedicated to fountain pens in 2011 and it has experienced rapid growth, now boasting over 30,000 members.

Fountain pens have proved popular enough that on November 2, 2012, Long Island retail supervisor Cary Yeager was able to declare an international Fountain Pen Day. Since then Yeager has added 48 sites to his list of participating pen blogs, with names like Gourmet Pens, the Well-Appointed Desk and the Gentleman Stationer.

Explanations for this revival vary. Wiederlight and others, including Scott Franklin, attribute it to the rise of the internet and social media.

“We see a whole youth movement in fountain pens and I think it’s because people are able to connect through the internet, through the blogs and Instagram and whatnot,” said Franklin. “It’s just easier to get exposed to things like this.”

But Jonathon Deans, an economics professor at the University of Newcastle in Australia who also runs a blog called Pen Economics, disagrees. “The dominant view from those guys seem to be that its a reaction against technology: So much is impersonal now that users want something analog, they want something they can do with their hands,” he said.

Deans doesn’t buy that argument. He credits, on the supply side, two major forces: improved technology, which allows manufacturers to produce fountain pens less prone to leaking, and lower prices. A few decades ago, a basic fountain pen might cost upwards of $100; these days a lower-end model from a reputable company like Lamy or Pilot can go for lest than $30.

Brad Dowdy, who runs a blog and podcast called The Pen Addict (its logo is a syringe with a fountain pen nib instead of a needle), has a different opinion. His blog receives about 1.2 million page views per month, while the podcast gets around 10,000 downloads per episode.

Fountain pens, he says, offer “ the most customizable writing experience you can get.”

“I have a writing style where I like to write with particular colors, or particular feel to the nib, or particular size of the line on the page or very specific type of paper that I like,” Dowdy explains. “And I could take all those things and find a specific pen, a specific ink, and a specific paper that speaks to Brad.”

***

One of the best places to find that specific pen, ink and paper is at a pen show. Weekend-long pen shows, the main events of the fountain pen world, draw retailers and manufacturers peddling their wares, sometimes including special events like auctions and seminars.

Though no definitive list seems to exist, pen hobbyist websites list 10 to 12 shows in the United States annually. This year’s first, the Philadelphia Pen Show, was under new management, which promised a show of firsts.

Eric Orozco, a California network tech who has attended over 20 shows, was taking over the show from Bert Oser, a long-time Maryland retailer, and he had high hopes.

Orozco had never organized a pen show before, or even attended the Philadelphia show. But he has been active in the fountain pen community for 15 years, running a popular online forum called FPGeeks and co-producing a bi-monthly fountain pen webcast called FPtv.

And he wasn’t worried by his inexperience. “All the vendors know what they’re doing; you don’t have to train anybody to do a pen show,” he said, a few weeks before the event. “They’ve all been doing it for years.”

Orozco had made some changes, though. The Philadelphia Pen Show, like most smaller shows, lacked an official website. Orozco created one, offering online tickets.

“This is the first pen show in the history of pen shows that has ever sold tickets for the pen show online,” said Orozco, sounding excited. “So far we’ve sold 200 online tickets, but I don’t if that’s a good thing or a bad thing because this is the first ever!” The record attendance at the show reached 1,000 people.

“Pen shows, if you like fountain pens, are amazing,” Orozco went on. “The room is full of not only fountain pens, but fountain pen people. You’ve found your tribe.”

At Franklin-Christoph, a co-organizer, people also sounded optimistic.

“Philadelphia has a lot of potential,” said Scott Franklin. “It can draw from the entire east coast. It’s also the opposite time of the year from the D.C. Pen Show,” one of the country’s two largest. “So a lot of people who go to D.C. in the summer will go to Philadelphia in the winter.”

But this year the show’s timing may have done more harm than good. At 10:30 AM, when Lei and Zhao arrived, the Westin ballroom was looking sparsely populated. Of the 56 vendors who had booked tables, the blizzard had proved too much of an obstacle for about a third, leaving empty tables scattered around the room. Orozco and Franklin had hoped for 800 to 1000 attendees; only about 300 came.

“This is the fewest people to ever come to the show,” sighed Detlef Bittner, owner of Bittner pens, a California retailer.

But that didn’t bother Lei. “The pen show was beyond my expectations. Of course it cost more money,” she said, laughing. “But it was even more fun than I thought it would be.”

Next up for Lei was Taccia pens, a boutique Japanese-manufactured pen brand specializing in higher-end fountain pens with unusual designs and exotic materials like buffalo horn. Lei had seen Taccia pens online and one model in particular had caught her eye: the Serpent Limited Edition, a black, hand-lacquered, cigar-shaped pen encircled by a sterling silver snake. Taccia had brought two versions to Philly: a white-gold plated snake and a yellow-gold one. Lei bought one of each.

Taccia’s founder, Shu-Jen Lin, who was manning the table, was so happy about Lei’s purchase that she offered both Lei and Zhao two ornately embroidered pen wraps, called “kimonos,” for free.

“Thank God for Anni,” Lin said, smiling as she packed up at the end of the show. Usually big rollers flew in from all over the country, but airport closures meant many had missed out on Philadelphia. Lin hadn’t quite recouped the cost of her flight and hotel stay, but Lei’s purchase made her day.

Then came Lei’s biggest buy of the show, the OMAS Montecristo. OMAS, a luxury Italian pen brand, is known for its imaginative limited edition pens. The Montecristo, based on the cigar, was particularly unusual in that its cap, with a hand-engraved sterling silver center ring, covered the whole pen body. The unique design appealed to Lei, but after dropping nearly $3,000 on the Taccia pens, she hesitated. Zhao, who had bought nothing but was there “for moral support only,” encouraged her: “You should get it if you really like it.”

“It usually goes for $1,900 but I can give it to you for $1,500,” said Bryan Hulser, vice president of Kenro Pens, OMAS’s US distributor. Lei took out her phone and converted the amount to Renminbi.

“If I buy this,” she said with a nervous smile, “I will have to drink wind and eat snow for the rest of the year.”

“Don’t worry,” Zhao said, laughing, “I will feed you.”

“There are only 400 of these in the whole world and this one is the prototype,” Hulser said, pointing to the 000 engraved on the cap lip. “I had to call the manufacturer to ask if we were allowed to sell this.”

Taking out her credit card, Lei said “I’ll take it.” Grinning, Hulser handed OMAS notebooks to both Lei and Zhao.

Lei’s last purchase, a limited edition Montegrappa pen commemorating the 15th Century War of the Roses was a bit of an anticlimax, at $870. But it brought a smile to Detlef Bittner, who sold her the pen. “I’ll polish it for you,” he said.

After that Lei seemed a little embarrassed. “I spent too much,” she said. “I should go now before I buy something else.” Less than two hours after arriving, she and Zhao left the ballroom to hike back through the snow to University City.

Since the show, Lei has enjoyed using her pens, but mostly from the safety of her room. She’ll take the Franklin-Christoph pens to class, but not the serpent pens, or the OMAS. “That would a little bit over the top,” she said, laughing.

***

The size of the contemporary fountain pen industry is unclear, even to industry members.

“Part of the problem is that fountain pen companies — whether it be manufacturers, distributors, retailers — all tend to be privately owned so they’re not publishing sales,” said Scott Franklin.

“Stationery as a whole is a very large market; fountain pens are a sizable chunk of the market by revenue,” said Jonathon Deans, who estimated that they accounted for perhaps five to 10 percent. According to a report by IBISWorld, an industry research company, the U.S. stationery market earned $2.2 billion in revenue in 2015, putting fountain pens at between $110 million to $220 million. “Fountain pens is a growth part of a declining sector. The relative importance of fountain pens is probably growing.”

Whatever the actual sales numbers, “the important thing it it’s something that’s getting more attention from brands in the stationery community,” Deans said. He cited the example of Staedtler, a German manufacturer that mostly sells drafting pencils but launched a fountain pen model last year. “The fact that they and other companies are investing in fountain pens is a sign that this industry is growing, the growth is healthy, and it’s attracting interest.”

Neither Franklin nor Deans think that fountain pens will ever become a more mainstream hobby. “I can’t imagine a case where the market would grow that much,” said Deans.

That hasn’t stopped people from continuing to enter the industry, however. In 2014, Brad Dowdy partnered with a friend to found Nock Co., which produces pen cases and other accessories. The vast majority of his customers are fountain pen users. By last December, the company had grown enough, with around 350 orders per month, to allow Dowdy to quit his IT day job and run Nock Co. full time.

“There’s new companies that are getting into” fountain pens and “the existing companies are always growing; they’re always hiring people,” Dowdy said. “I might have rose-colored glasses because I’m in it, but to me it feels good, it feels healthy.”

Deans thinks that a big reason is the community. Once someone buys a fountain pen, the chance to spend time with fellow aficionados keeps them coming back for more. “It’s not just the fact that you enjoy using your pens,” he said. “It’s that the pens connect you to this community of people who are just incredibly nice and friendly and funny and great to engage with.”

***

The diversity in backgrounds and tastes among fountain pen lovers can be found in pen clubs across the world, including one in Manhattan.

At one recent meeting, about a dozen people mostly white-haired men sat in a circle of metal chairs in the public atrium of the Olympic Towers on Fifth Avenue, right across from St. Patrick’s Cathedral. With no signs or identification, they forced potential new members to approach and ask, “Is this the Big Apple Pen Club?” Then the circle would widen, chairs scraping against the tiled floor.

The Big Apple Pen Club meets every other month, at various locations around Manhattan. The November meetup celebrated Fountain Pen Day, created by Long Island pen enthusiast Cary Yeager.

Club President Josh Lax, a lawyer from Brooklyn, opened the meeting by asking everyone to introduce themselves. Some intros were terse (“I’m Mitch, I’ve been here before.”) Most were enthusiastic and self-deprecating.

Wendy Williams, a corporate secretary and long-time member, described herself as having “compulsive acquisition disorder.” Chris Kelly, a Merrill Lynch banker and first-time attendee, said he buys “way too many” fountain pens, dismaying his wife, who hoped that this meeting would be “like Fountain Pen Addicts Anonymous.”

Tori Coke, another long-time member and proud owner of a complete collection of Lamy Safaris and Al-Stars (42 in all), wore a dark grey T-shirt whose white lettering seemed all the introduction she needed: “You can’t buy happiness but you can buy a fountain pen and that’s pretty much the same thing.”

Introductions made, Lax announced an ink raffle — the club had received some free bottles from Pelikan, a German pen company. He also handed around Fountain Pen Day swag — pins and bookmarks with Yeager’s Fountain Pen Day logo. Then he invited everyone to help themselves to the mini cupcakes and red wine.

That announcement marked the end of the official Big Apple Pen Club program, a mere 15 minutes after it began. People started happily pulling out their pen cases and spent the next two and a half hours admiring each other’s collections.

Richie Fernandez, who buys and resells vintage pens online under the name Las Plumas, had brought one of his rarer finds, an intricately carved wooden dip pen with a tiny hole that, held to the light, depicted the 1893 Chicago World’s Fair.

Eleanor Innes, a first-timer, brought assorted ink bottles of various colors and brands, plus a boxful of empty ink vials and a pair of syringes that called to mind a chemistry experiment, or drug paraphernalia. Innes generously offered ink samples to everyone, ranging from the unimaginative Lamy Blue to the fancier-sounding Diamine Syrah to the inexplicably-named Shimmertastic Brandy Dazzle.

Dan Hoizner, an IT tech and one of the younger members, proved a particularly popular attraction, with four or five people huddled around him. His collection included a number of uncommon modern pens, including a Nakaya, a hand-lacquered Japanese pen that cost upwards of a thousand dollars. Lenny Schildkraut, a licensed counselor who works for the city and only owns two pens, one a gift, looked a little taken aback at the price.

Wendy Williams, on the other hand, handled the pen lovingly, a trace of wistfulness in her eyes. “I keep meaning to take the plunge,” she said. “Maybe for Christmas.”

***

If the weather hindered the Philadelphia show, it proved helpful to this year’s Long Island Pen Show, held at Hofstra University in Hempstead.

Organized by two stalwarts of the Long Island pen scene, Terry Brack and Mike Bloom, the March show usually takes place in more wintery weather, but sunny skies and temperatures in the 60s greeted this year’s attendees.

The show attracted more than 1,000 people, Brack estimated, a sizable increase from last year’s 600 to 700.

Brack, a Hofstra chemistry professor expected the surge. Newsday, the local daily, had published a two-page feature on the show a week before.

One of the smaller pen gatherings the Long Island show has grown steadily since Brack started it in 2006 as an alternative to the now-defunct New York City Pen Show.

“The first show was very small, only a couple hundred people,” said Brack, “But every year has been bigger than the previous one.”

The show also boasted a number of first-time vendors including Franklin-Christoph and two members of the Big Apple Pen Club: Josh Lax and Yoshi Nakama, who shared a table.

Scott Franklin had been undecided about coming to Long Island, but at the urging of customers and fellow vendors, sent nibmeister Jim Rouse on a solo mission straight from the Baltimore show a week before. The Franklin-Christoph table was smaller and less well-stocked than at Philly, but that didn’t stop customers from swarming it.

Things got so busy that Rouse, occupied with fine-tuning customers’ nibs, enlisted the help of Big Apple members and Franklin-Christoph aficionados Dan Hoizner and Eleanor Innes, who jumped into action behind the table, explaining pen models and nib sizes with a level of detail that matched any employee’s.

“We’re not with Franklin-Christoph, but we’ll do our best to help you,” said Innes, a bank executive from Jackson Heights, laughing, to one group of buyers.

“These are the prototypes that are made with special acrylics,” said Hoizner, a Staten Island software engineer, pointing out a tray near the end of the table. “So they’re really one of a kind.”

The display drew so many customers that Lax momentarily abandoned his own post to reserve one of the prototypes with an all-orange cap and barrel.

Lax, the club president, had rented the table for his newly-formed business, the J.J. Lax Pen Company, which customizes pens and grinds nibs? Lax, who plunged headfirst into the hobby in 2009 became fascinated with restoring vintage lever-filling pens. After taking a class from Richard Binder, America’s foremost nibmeister, in 2012 (even missing his first wedding anniversary), Lax became more and more interested in nib grinding. In 2014, he began sitting with Binder during pen shows to pick up the tricks of the trade.

“Through working with Richard, I acquired the skills,” said Lax. “He has a lot of knowledge and a lot of technique.”

Although he had a dry run at the previous month’s Big Apple meeting, where he offered members discounted nib grinding, this was Lax’s first time as a pen show vendor. His table, located in a corner of the student center near the wall, was chock full of intimidating-looking equipment and sample pens that showcased his nibworking expertise.

Lax’s set-up included tools originally meant for jewelers and metal-workers, like a rotary tool for the actual nib grinding and shaping, which looks like a drill without a bit, and a flexible shaft for polishing nibs, which emits sounds reminiscent of a dental practice.

“Reminds me that I missed my last appointment,” said Wendy Williams, another Big Apple Pen Club member who had volunteered to help Lax. At the Baltimore Pen Show the previous weekend, she’d gotten tips from Binder’s wife on being a good “mama nibmeister” and making sure Lax paced himself and didn’t spend too much time with one customer.

Nib grinding can be time consuming. At his table, Lax first observed how customers write, watching for the angles at which they hold their pens. Then he makes the desired adjustments, whether transforming the nib entirely from a round point to an italic one or just tuning it to write more smoothly.

“He’s just so generous with his time and knowledge,” said Williams, after observing Lax work with a customer for over half an hour. “He wants to make sure that everything’s perfect.”

One happy customer was Tori Coke, another club member and Lax’s first client on Sunday. She wanted him to grind her newly-purchased TWSBI into an oblique nib; to illustrate, she showed him a pen ground by Richard Binder himself. “I can do that,” Lax said, mock-arrogantly, taking on the challenge.

And, after 15 minutes, he succeeded. “Ah!” squealed Coke in delight, trying the newly-ground pen. “It’s perfect!”

But Lax also turned away potential customers if he didn’t think he could help them. “I don’t really see anything wrong with it,” he told a man who wanted his nib smoothed, examining his nib through a loupe. “If it ain’t broke, don’t fix it.”

Nakama’s was also making his pen show vendor debut, offering hand-turned pens for sale. A native of Japan, Nakama came to New York in 1979 and worked as a fabricator for artist Sol LeWitt for over two decades. He started making pens in 2012 and just launched his company, Pen 18111, this year.

Most of Nakama’s pens are Japanese-influenced, with cigar shapes and laser-engraved leaf and floral patterns similar to many Japanese pen manufacturers’. But one of his more unusual pens garnered the most attention.

“What is this made of?” asked one browser, holding up a greyish pen with a cap shaped like one of the giant heads on Easter Island. The material looked rocky and felt rough to the touch. “It’s 3D-printed,” answered Nakama, garnering appreciative oohs and aahs from onlookers.

Nakama only sold two pens, each around $150, over the two-day show (including the 3D-printed one), but wasn’t too disappointed. “I just wanted to get my name card out there,” he said, smiling, pointing to his stack of business cards, each illustrated with a different hand-made pen.

Lax was also satisfied, though he had hoped to be a little busier. “I’m glad I did it,” he said. “It was a lot of fun!” He’ll “definitely” work another show, he said, but with a year-old baby at home and his day job as an attorney he’s not sure when. “For the foreseeable future this is a side gig for me.”

As for the Long Island Pen Show as a whole, Brack considered it a resounding success and is considering expansion. “We could rent a smaller room and run workshops out of it,” he said. “Or we could have an auction on Saturday night to bring people back in.”

On fountain pen community’s future, Brack sounded cautiously optimistic. “We need more young people to start to take over,” he said. “And we’re starting to see that.”
